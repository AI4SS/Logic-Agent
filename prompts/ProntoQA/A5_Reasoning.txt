You are a symbolic reasoning expert skilled in logic-based natural language understanding.

Your task is to determine whether the proposition **S1** from a Semiotic Square is **True** or **False**, based on:

1. Natural language premises (facts, subclass chains, properties)
2. Their corresponding First-Order Logic (FOL) forms
3. Four propositions from a Semiotic Square: S1, S2, ¬S1, ¬S2 (each with both statement and FOL)

------
Your job is to assess whether S1 can be logically **entailed** or **refuted**, based on chaining subclass relationships and entity properties through the FOL structure.

------
## Determination Rules

- **S1 is True** if all of the following conditions are met:
  1. There exists at least one **valid reasoning chain** from the premises that **logically entails** the predicate in S1.
     - A valid reasoning chain is a series of subclass or property inheritance steps (e.g., A(x) → B(x) → C(x)) where the final predicate matches S1's conclusion.
  2. There is **no reasoning chain** that leads to **S2**.
     - This means the contrary statement of S1 cannot be derived using the same reasoning mechanisms.
  3. There is **no chain** that leads to **¬S1**.
     - That is, no negation of S1’s predicate is derivable through subclass/property inversion.

- **S1 is False** if **any** of the following is true:
  1. A reasoning chain leads directly to **S2**.
     - That is, from entity membership and subclass/property chains, one can derive the conclusion stated in S2.
  2. A reasoning chain proves **¬S1**.
     - This occurs if, starting from the subject entity, the negation of S1's predicate is reached through logical inheritance or definition.
  3. **No valid reasoning chain exists to derive S1**, and no contradiction blocks the derivation of S2 or ¬S1.
     - That is, if S1 cannot be proven true from any combination of premises, then it must be labeled as False.

- Important:
  - A reasoning chain is only valid if:
    - All steps are justified by premises (subclass or property assertions)
    - The entity involved is explicitly or transitively linked via subclass relations to the final predicate
  - You must always prefer definitive chains over ambiguity.
  - Do not assume missing links; if a chain cannot be constructed, S1 is not entailed.

- “Uncertain” is disallowed:
  - If S1 cannot be derived, it is treated as False.
  - Only derivation of S1 with no contradiction validates True.

Always combine subclass and attribute chains when necessary.

Below is an example:

**Question:** Is Max sour?

**Premises:**

Max is a yumpus.
FOL: IsA(Max, Yumpus)

Each yumpus is a dumpus.
FOL: ∀x (Yumpus(x) → Dumpus(x))

Each dumpus is a wumpus.
FOL: ∀x (Dumpus(x) → Wumpus(x))

Every wumpus is an impus.
FOL: ∀x (Wumpus(x) → Impus(x))

Each impus is a tumpus.
FOL: ∀x (Impus(x) → Tumpus(x))

Tumpuses are not sour.
FOL: ∀x (Tumpus(x) → ¬Sour(x))

**Semiotic Square:**
S1: Max is sour.
FOL: Sour(Max)

S2: Max is not sour.
FOL: ¬Sour(Max)

¬S1: Max is not sour.
FOL: ¬Sour(Max)

¬S2: Max is sour.
FOL: Sour(Max)

**Reasoning:**

Max is a yumpus.

By inheritance: Yumpus → Dumpus → Wumpus → Impus → Tumpus

Tumpus implies ¬Sour

So Max is a Tumpus ⇒ ¬Sour(Max)

Therefore, S1 (Sour(Max)) is False.
